What actually moves life expectancy?

An analysis of WHO Global Health Observatory data across 193 countries from 2000 to 2015 — tracking how life expectancy shifted, where the biggest gaps remain, and which factors correlate most strongly with longer lives.

By Savitha G S · pandas · Chart.js · dataset cleaned & imputed from source

Life expectancy is rising — but not evenly

Global average life expectancy climbed from 66.8 to 71.6 years between 2000 and 2015. Toggle between development status and region to see how the trend breaks down.

Regional snapshot, 2015

Africa's average sits more than 16 years below Europe's — the single largest structural gap in the dataset.

What correlates with a longer life

Schooling and income composition track life expectancy more closely than GDP does — education and how resources are used matter more than raw wealth. Adult mortality and HIV/AIDS prevalence are the strongest negative correlates.

    Methodology & notes

    • Source WHO Global Health Observatory life expectancy dataset (2000–2015, 193 countries), compiled with UN economic data.
    • Cleaning Missing values interpolated within each country's own time series first; remaining gaps filled using the median for that country's development status and year, then a global fallback.
    • Enrichment Countries mapped to continent-level regions to support regional comparison, since the source file only listed country names.
    • Caveat Slovenia's reported 2015 figure (88.0 years) is a known outlier in this source file, well above independently reported values (~80–81 years). It's shown as-is with this note rather than silently corrected, since altering source data without a verified reference isn't good practice.

    About this project

    Built to demonstrate an end-to-end analytics workflow: sourcing a real public dataset, cleaning and imputing missing values, engineering a regional feature, computing correlations, and communicating findings through an interactive dashboard.
